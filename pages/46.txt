      <p><b>General Foods Corporation</b> was a company whose direct predecessor was established in the United States by <a href="/wiki/C._W._Post" title="C. W. Post">Charles William Post</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Postum_Cereal_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Postum Cereal Company">Postum Cereal Company</a> in 1895. The name "General Foods" was adopted in 1929, after several corporate acquisitions, by <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Merriweather_Post" title="Marjorie Merriweather Post">Marjorie Post</a> after she inherited the established cereal business from her father <a href="/wiki/C._W._Post" title="C. W. Post">C. W. Post</a>. In November 1985, General Foods was acquired by Philip Morris Companies (now <a href="/wiki/Altria" title="Altria">Altria</a> Group, Inc.) for $5.6 billion, the largest non-oil acquisition to that time. In December 1988, Philip Morris acquired <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods_Inc." title="Kraft Foods Inc.">Kraft Foods Inc.</a>, and, in 1990, combined the two food companies as <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft Foods</a>. "General Foods" was dropped from the corporate name in 1995; a line of caffeinated hot beverage mixes continued to carry the <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_House_International" title="Maxwell House International">General Foods International</a> name until 2010. </p>

      <p>Roots of General Foods can be traced to the <a href="/wiki/Post_Consumer_Brands" title="Post Consumer Brands">Post Cereal Company</a>, founded by <a href="/wiki/C._W._Post" title="C. W. Post">C. W. Post</a> in 1895 in <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek,_Michigan" title="Battle Creek, Michigan">Battle Creek, Michigan</a>. Post was a patient at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_Creek_Sanitarium" title="Battle Creek Sanitarium">Battle Creek Sanitarium</a> (run by <a href="/wiki/John_Harvey_Kellogg" title="John Harvey Kellogg">John Harvey Kellogg</a>, brother of <a href="/wiki/Kellogg%27s" title="Kellogg's">Kellogg Company</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Will_Keith_Kellogg" title="Will Keith Kellogg">Will Keith Kellogg</a>), and was inspired by the diet there to start his food company (and become a rival to the Kellogg brothers, who sold their own breakfast cereals). Post invested $78 in his initial equipment and supplies and set up manufacturing in a barn on what was known as the 'Old Beardsley Farm'.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> His first product was <a href="/wiki/Postum" title="Postum">Postum</a>, a "cereal beverage" alternative to coffee made from wheat and molasses. The first cereal, <a href="/wiki/Grape-Nuts" title="Grape-Nuts">Grape-Nuts</a>, was developed in 1897 followed by Elijah's Manna in 1904 which was renamed Post Toasties in 1908.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>In 1907 <i><a href="/wiki/Collier%27s" title="Collier's">Collier's</a> Weekly</i> published an article questioning the claim made in advertisements for Grape Nuts that it could cure <a href="/wiki/Appendicitis" title="Appendicitis">appendicitis</a>. C. W. Post responded with advertisements questioning the mental capacity of the article's author, and <i>Collier's Weekly</i> sued for libel. The case was heard in 1910, and Post was fined $50,000. The decision was overturned on appeal, but advertisements for Postum products stopped making such claims.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> </p> 
      <div style="clear:both;"></div> 
      <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_Foods">General Foods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=General_Foods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General Foods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
      <p>After C.W. Post died in 1914, his daughter <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Merriweather_Post" title="Marjorie Merriweather Post">Marjorie Merriweather Post</a> took over the company. The Postum Cereals company, which went on with some acquisitions such as <a href="/wiki/Jell-O" title="Jell-O">Jell-O</a> (gelatin dessert) in 1925, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Baker_%26_Company" title="Walter Baker &amp; Company">Walter Baker &amp; Company</a> (chocolate) in 1927, <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_House" title="Maxwell House">Maxwell House</a> (coffee) in 1928, and other food brands. </p>
      <p>By far the most important acquisition of 1929 was of the frozen-food company owned by <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Birdseye" title="Clarence Birdseye">Clarence Birdseye</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> called "General Seafood Corporation". Birdseye (December 9, 1886 - October 7, 1956) was one of the most important entrepreneurs in the history of the food industry. Born in New York City, he became interested in the frozen preservation of food during the course of working as a fur trader in <a href="/wiki/Labrador" title="Labrador">Labrador</a> between 1912 and 1916. By 1923, he had developed a commercially viable process for <a href="/wiki/Flash_freezing" title="Flash freezing">quick-freezing</a> foods using a belt mechanism, which he patented. In 1924, with backing from three investors, he formed the "General Seafoods Company", in <a href="/wiki/Gloucester,_Massachusetts" title="Gloucester, Massachusetts">Gloucester, Massachusetts</a>, to produce frozen <a href="/wiki/Haddock" title="Haddock">haddock</a> fillets packed in plain cardboard boxes. </p>

      <p>The founder's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Marjorie_Merriweather_Post" title="Marjorie Merriweather Post">Marjorie Merriweather Post</a>, was first to become excited about the prospects for the frozen foods business. In 1926, she had put into port at Gloucester on her yacht, <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Cloud" title="Sea Cloud">Sea Cloud</a></i>, and was served a luncheon meal which, she learned to her amazement, had been frozen six months before. Despite her enthusiasm, it took Post three years to convince Postum's management to acquire the company. Postum paid $10.75 million for a 51% interest and its partner, <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs" title="Goldman Sachs">Goldman Sachs</a>, paid $12.5 million for the other 49%.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Following this acquisition, Postum, Inc changed its name to General Foods Corporation. Goldman sold its share back to General Foods in 1932, apparently at a slight loss. </p>
      <p>Shortly after the acquisition, General Foods began test-marketing an expanded line of <a href="/wiki/Frozen_foods" class="mw-redirect" title="Frozen foods">frozen foods</a>, but the company quickly realized that a packaging process alone would not be sufficient to market frozen products in stores. To be sold, the packages had to be kept frozen while on display, so Birdseye engineers began development of a freezer cabinet designed specifically to hold frozen foods. The cabinet, which first appeared in 1934, required a great deal of space and electricity, which were not readily available in most grocery stores of the period. For those stores which could accommodate them, the payback was immediate. Housewives quickly realized that keeping packages of frozen food in the icebox could mean fresher meals and fewer trips to the market.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>The company published a cook book in 1932, the <i>General Foods Cook Book</i>, dedicated "To the American homemaker". Five editions were published between 1932 and 1937. The book includes photographs (among which is "General Foods offers over twenty famous products for your well-stocked pantry shelf") and a subject index.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> </p>
      <p>General Foods was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Altria_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Altria Group">Philip Morris Companies</a> in 1985. In 1989, Philip Morris merged General Foods with <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods_Inc." title="Kraft Foods Inc.">Kraft Foods Inc.</a>, which it had acquired in 1987, to form the "<a href="/wiki/Kraft_Foods" title="Kraft Foods">Kraft General Foods</a>" division. The cereal brands of <a href="/wiki/Nabisco" title="Nabisco">Nabisco</a> were acquired in 1993. In 1995, Kraft General Foods was reorganized and renamed "Kraft Foods". On November 15, 2007, Kraft announced it would spin off Post Cereals and merge that business with <a href="/wiki/Ralcorp" title="Ralcorp">Ralcorp Holdings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Merger_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merger-8">[8]</a></sup> That merger was completed August 4, 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-Ralcorp_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ralcorp-9">[9]</a></sup> at which time the official name of the company became Post Foods, LLC. </p>
